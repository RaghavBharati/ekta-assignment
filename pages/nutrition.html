Nutrition & Healthy Eating

Fuel your study sessions, lectures and late nights with food that actually works for a student budget and a student timetable.

The basics

Build a balanced plate

You don't need fancy meal plans or expensive supplements to eat well. The easiest habit to master is the balanced plate — a simple way to portion any meal by sight, no scales or apps required.

Picture your plate split into three parts. Fill it in these proportions and most of your nutrition takes care of itself, whether you're cooking in a shared kitchen or grabbing food between classes.

  • ½ vegetables & fruit — the biggest portion. Colour, fibre, vitamins and volume that keeps you full for fewer calories. Frozen and tinned count too.
  • ¼ protein — eggs, chicken, fish, tofu, beans, lentils or Greek yoghurt. Protein steadies energy and stops the 3 p.m. snack crash.
  • ¼ wholegrains — brown rice, wholemeal pasta, oats or wholegrain bread for slow-release energy that lasts through long lectures.
  • A little healthy fat — a drizzle of olive oil, a handful of nuts or half an avocado to help you absorb vitamins.

Cook it yourself

Quick, healthy student recipes

Four go-to meals that are cheap, fast and forgiving. Swap ingredients for whatever's on offer — these are guidelines, not rules.

Avocado Toast

Creamy, filling and ready before the kettle boils. A brilliant breakfast or lazy lunch.

🕒 5 min 💰 Budget 🌱 Vegan
  • 2 slices wholegrain bread
  • 1 ripe avocado
  • Lemon, chilli flakes, salt
  • Optional: pumpkin seeds

Overnight Oats

Prep it in a jar tonight, grab it tomorrow. Zero morning effort and no cooking at all.

🕒 5 min prep 💰 Budget 🍳 No-cook
  • ½ cup rolled oats
  • ½ cup milk or plant milk
  • 1 spoon yoghurt
  • Fruit + honey to top

Buddha Bowl

A balanced plate in a bowl. Batch-cook the base and mix up the toppings all week.

🕒 20 min 🌱 Vegan ⚡ High protein
  • Cooked rice or quinoa
  • Tin of chickpeas
  • Any roast or raw veg
  • Tahini or yoghurt dressing

Berry Smoothie

Breakfast you can drink on the walk to campus, powered by cheap frozen fruit.

🕒 3 min 💰 Budget ⚡ High protein
  • 1 cup frozen berries
  • 1 banana
  • 1 cup milk or plant milk
  • Spoon of oats or peanut butter
Everyday habits

Nutrition tips for students

Small, sustainable changes beat strict diets every time. Pick one or two to start with.

🍱

Meal prep in batches

Cook once, eat three times. Make a big pot of rice, pasta or curry at the weekend and portion it into containers so a healthy meal is always ready when deadlines hit.

🥜

Snack smart

Keep filling snacks within reach — nuts, fruit, yoghurt or wholegrain crackers. When something good is easy to grab, you're far less likely to raid the vending machine.

💧

Stay hydrated

Tiredness and headaches are often just dehydration. Carry a refillable bottle and sip through the day — your focus in lectures will thank you.

🏷️

Read the labels

Check the per-100g column to compare products, and watch for hidden sugar and salt. It takes ten seconds and quickly makes you a savvier shopper.

💷

Eat well on a budget

Beans, lentils, eggs, oats and frozen veg are nutrition powerhouses for pennies. Shop the reduced shelf, buy own-brand and plan meals around what's cheap that week.

☕

Balance your caffeine

Coffee helps, but too much late in the day wrecks your sleep and next-day energy. Cap it at the afternoon and swap the extra cup for water or herbal tea.

💧 Don't forget to drink up

How much water do you actually need?

Most students walk around mildly dehydrated without realising it — and it shows up as tiredness, poor concentration and headaches. A good rule of thumb is around 2 litres a day, more when it's hot or you've been active.

Not sure what's right for you? Use the water intake calculator on our resources page to get a personalised daily target, then log your glasses on your dashboard to build the habit.
